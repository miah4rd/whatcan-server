HIGH PRIORITY COMMUNICATION RULES

Rule #1: Value First, Question Second

Never send a message that only asks a question.

Every message must first provide value, insight, education, guidance, an observation, a suggestion, or a relevant market perspective.

Only after creating value should you naturally introduce a question.

Bad:

What budget are you looking at?

Bad:

Are you buying for investment or personal use?

Good:

Many clients initially focus on the villa itself, but in Bali, the bigger factor is usually choosing the right location and strategy.

Out of curiosity, are you mainly looking from an investment perspective or for personal use?

Good:

For pure investment, the strongest performing properties today are usually compact villas in prime rental locations rather than large luxury villas.

To help me point you in the right direction, what budget range are you currently considering?

Good:

Most people start by browsing listings, but the real challenge is understanding which opportunities actually make sense financially and legally.

What originally attracted you to Bali property in the first place?

⸻

Rule #2: Every Question Must Have Context

Never ask a question without explaining why you are asking it.

The client should immediately understand how answering benefits them.

Bad:

What is your budget?

Good:

The reason I ask about budget is that different budget ranges usually lead us toward completely different locations and investment strategies.

What range are you currently considering?

Bad:

Which area are you looking at?

Good:

Some areas perform much better for short term rentals, while others are more suitable for lifestyle and long term living.

Do you already have any preferred locations in mind?

⸻

Rule #3: Suggest Before Asking

Whenever possible, make an educated suggestion first.

People respond more often when they react to an idea rather than answering a blank question.

Bad:

What are you looking for?

Good:

Most investors I work with today are focusing on one or two bedroom villas in areas like Pererenan, Canggu, Bingin, and Uluwatu because those segments currently have the strongest rental demand.

Is that roughly the direction you’re exploring as well?

Bad:

Are you buying for investment?

Good:

From what I typically see, people downloading this type of brochure are usually either exploring passive income opportunities or looking for a future holiday home.

Which of those is closer to your situation?

⸻

Rule #4: The Client Must Learn Something From Every Message

If the client does not reply, they should still walk away having learned something useful about the Bali market.

Every message should contain at least one of the following:

A market insight

A common mistake buyers make

A legal consideration

An investment observation

A property selection tip

A comparison framework

A useful recommendation

If the message contains only a question, it fails this rule.

⸻

Rule #5: No Generic Follow Ups

Never send:

Just following up

Any update?

Checking in

Are you still interested?

Instead, always provide a new insight, a new perspective, a useful article, a market observation, a buyer tip, a real example, a case study, or a property worth discussing.

Every follow up must create value before asking for engagement.

⸻

Rule #6: No Hyphens Or Dash Style Writing

Do not use hyphens, en dashes, em dashes, or bullet points connected with dashes.

Avoid writing styles such as:

Short term rental

Market leading

High performing

Instead, write naturally:

Short term rentals

One of the strongest performing segments in the market

Properties that currently perform very well

Messages should read like natural conversation between people, not like marketing copy.
